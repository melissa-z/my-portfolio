The Microwave
How do we perceive and interact with microwaves?

The microwave oven is a common appliance that heats up foods quickly, and its interface seeks to provide an effortless, mess-free, and hands-off cooking experience.

But is that the reality of users' experiences? I conducted some user research to find out, and created two personas and a storyboard that could help to inform a better microwave design.

Sketch

To investigate how people perceive and interact with microwaves, I began with a quick sketch to note its key components.

Research

I observed three users using microwaves in their everyday lives. I then interviewed them with a list of questions to gain insight about their experience, expectations, motives, and challenges while using the interface.

Questions

  1. On average, how many times a day do you use the microwave?
  2. What time(s) of day do you use the microwave?
  3. What kinds of foods do you use the microwave for?
  4. While the microwave is running, how do you spend your time?
  5. Which buttons on the microwave do you use most often?
  6. Are there buttons on the microwave that you’ve never used?
  7. How satisfied are you with the microwave's efficiency?
  8. How satisfied are you with the microwave's results? Do you find it necessary to add more time or take food out early?
  9. Do you clean your microwave? If yes, how often?
  10. How do you feel about the process of cooking in general?

Celine

Celine is a mother of two who cooks for the whole family.

Celine uses the microwave to heat up leftovers. She uses the number pad to set time quickly and decisively, but hesitates just before pressing start. She leaves to get a napkin to cover the plate, and then starts the microwave.

While microwave is running, Celine stretches her legs, then checks her phone for messages.

Celine is at first unsatisfied by the food's temperature, and adds an extra 20 seconds of cooking time.

Celine usually uses the microwave 2 to 3 times a day, and finds it quick to use and easy to clean up after. Celine says that while she enjoys cooking, she finds it time consuming.

However, Celine finds it difficult to estimate the heating time needed, and often needs to add more time to get the desired temperature.

There are many buttons on the microwave that Celine does not find useful. She only uses "start" and the number pad to set time. She does not use any of the other buttons (eg. power level, preset cooking modes) – she is not sure what they do, and isn't interested in finding out.

Momoka

Momoka is a college student living at home.

Momoka microwaves a bowl of instant ramen for lunch. She pours water into the ramen bowl, reads the instructions on the packaging, and quickly enters in the instructed time.

While the ramen cooks, she does some squats in the kitchen, and then leans against the counter and does some counter push-ups.

When the microwave timer is up, she carefully lifts the steaming noodles out, struggling to carry the bowl because of the heat.

Momoka says that she wants to learn to cook, but doesn’t do it often because it is time consuming. She finds the microwave to be a fast and convenient cooking appliance, particularly for days where she has little time betweeen classes.

The only buttons that she uses are the number pads, start, and power level. She has never used settings for prepackaged foods (eg. frozen meals, pizza), despite microwaving prepackaged foods often.

Rafael

Rafael is a college student living off-campus on his own.

Rafael grabs a frozen pre-made burrito from the freezer and puts it directly into the microwave. He quickly and decisively sets the time, changes the power level, and then presses the start button.

While microwave is running, leans on kitchen counter and checks his phone – reads emails, news, then plays game.

When the microwave beeps, he immediately takes out the burrito but burns his hands a little, and tosses the burrito around like a hot potato.

Rafael describes cooking as “not my favorite”. On average, he uses the microwave 2-4 a day for lunch, dinner, and snacks. He doesn’t usually know exactly how long to microwave items for, and adds more time as he goes.

His most used buttons are the power-level button, the “set 1min” shortcut, and start. He never uses any preset buttons for frozen meals, even when cooking frozen meals, and he also finds the light button unnecessary.

Personas

From the data collected through observations and interviews, I constructed two personas with empathy maps: On-the-go Owen and Careful Carrie.

Storyboard

Based on On-the-go Owen's persona, I created a storyboard that depicts his complete journey interacting with a microwave, including the context of the interaction as well as the challenges that he faces.

Conclusions

It seems that while users generally agree on the efficiency and ease of microwave cooking, the interface also contains many options and buttons that are not well understood and rarely used - perhaps its interface could be further simplified, and its unintuitive functionalities made more learnable.

I gained a much deeper understanding of users' interaction patterns with microwaves through this research, and the personas and the storyboard can help to inform better designs for a microwave that is more reflective of how people use it in everyday life.
